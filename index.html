Now, you may ask me that where is the proof that every even number greater than two can be expressed as the sum of two primes. See the generalisations below:

GENERALISATION

Suppose you start your prime sequence P' with (n + 1)th term and another sequence P with nth term.Now you do following operations on elements of both sequence:

P + P' = {even, odd}(i.e set of even and odd integers)

P - P' = {even, odd}

Let P + P' = Q, and P - P' = Q'

(P x P') + (Q x Q') = k

Now, 'k' can be a prime number and if it is not a prime number then it has atmost two factor 'a' and 'b'

If 'k' is not a prime number then,

Factors of PxP' and QxQ' comprises of:

$${2^{a0}}*{3^{a1}}*{5^{a2}}... $$

Here, a + a0 + a1 +... = N-{{1},{2}}

In above, it says that any even integer can be expressed as sum of two prime numbers.

Example 1:

$$(35, 24) = {2^{3}}*{3^{1}}*{5^{1}}*{7^{1}}$$

Here, $$3 + 1 + 1 + 1 = 6$$ $$3 + 3 = 6$$

Example 2:

$$(77, 72) = {2^{3}}*{3^{2}}*{11^{1}}*{7^{1}}$$

Here, $$3 + 2 + 1 + 1 = 7$$ $$3 + 4 = 7$$ $$3 = 7 - 4$$ $$3 + 2 + 2 = 7$$
